## 1) How this repo uses J2 (where it lives)

### 1.1 `Orbital_Docking_Optimizer.py` is just the runner

`Orbital_Docking_Optimizer.py` calls `optimize_all_segment_counts(...)` from the package and does not implement/toggle J2 itself.

Key import/call sites:

- `Orbital_Docking_Optimizer.py`:
  - imports `optimize_all_segment_counts` from `orbital_docking`
  - calls it for each curve order / segment count

### 1.2 J2 is implemented in the optimizer dynamics model

J2 is included in the gravitational acceleration model inside:

- `orbital_docking/optimization.py`
  - `_accel_j2(r_km, mu_km3_s2, r_e_km, j2)`
  - `_accel_total(...) = _accel_two_body(...) + _accel_j2(...)`

and the optimizer objective is built by linearizing **two-body + J2** gravity along the current reference curve:

- `orbital_docking/optimization.py`
  - `_build_ctrl_accel_quadratic(P_ref, T, sample_count)`
    - uses:
      - `mu = constants.EARTH_MU_SCALED`
      - `r_e = constants.EARTH_RADIUS_KM`
      - `j2 = constants.EARTH_J2`
    - forms `f_total = _accel_total(...)`
    - computes a numeric Jacobian `J = _jacobian_numeric(f_total, r_ref)`
    - builds a quadratic cost in the Bézier control points based on the linearization
  - `optimize_orbital_docking(...)`
    - SCP-style outer loop updates:
      - KOZ linearization (constraints)
      - gravity+J2 linearization (objective)

## 2) What J2 means (ELI5)

Earth is not a perfect sphere; it’s slightly “squished” (equatorial bulge).

That shape makes Earth’s gravitational pull deviate a little from the perfect-sphere model.

**J2 is the main number that captures that bulge effect** in a simple correction term.

---

## 3) What “coefficient of Earth’s 2nd-degree zonal spherical harmonic” means (ELI18)

### 3.1 Gravity potential as “simple + corrections”

Earth’s gravitational potential can be expressed as:

- a spherical (two-body) part, plus
- a sum of structured correction patterns over the sphere.

Those correction patterns are expressed using **spherical harmonics**, which form an orthogonal basis on the sphere (analogous to how sines/cosines form a basis for periodic 1D signals in Fourier series).

### 3.2 “Degree” and “zonal”

Spherical harmonics are indexed by \((\ell, m)\):

- **degree** \(\ell\): how “complex” the pattern is (more bands / smaller features as \(\ell\) increases)
- **order** \(m\): how much it varies with longitude

“**zonal**” means **axisymmetric** (no longitude dependence) → \(m = 0\).

### 3.3 Why \(\ell = 2\) is special (quadrupole / oblateness)

\(\ell = 0\) is the spherical monopole (perfect sphere).

\(\ell = 2, m = 0\) is the first dominant correction for an oblate planet and corresponds to a “quadrupole-like” latitude pattern (captures equator-vs-pole differences).

### 3.4 “Coefficient”

Once you choose a basis of patterns (spherical harmonics), you need a scalar multiplier for each basis term.

That scalar is the **coefficient**.

So:

- “2nd-degree zonal spherical harmonic” = the \((\ell=2, m=0)\) basis pattern
- **J2** = the coefficient multiplying that pattern in the potential expansion

### 3.5 Consequence for orbits (high level)

The J2 term causes slow, cumulative orbital element changes, especially:

- nodal (RAAN) precession
- argument of perigee precession

These effects are stronger at lower altitude (scales with \((R_e/r)^2\) in the potential correction).

---

## 4) Practical note for this repo

In this codebase, J2 is not a separate “feature flag” in the top-level runner; it is built into the optimizer’s gravity model and therefore influences the optimization objective whenever `_build_ctrl_accel_quadratic(...)` is used.
